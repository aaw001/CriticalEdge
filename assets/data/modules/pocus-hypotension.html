Module

Prehospital POCUS for the Hypotensive Patient

Differentiate shock fast and route care smarter.

~30–45 min Level: Intermediate Audience: Paramedics, HEMS, prehospital clinicians

Learning outcomes

By the end you can:

  • Run a focused hypotension POCUS pathway that narrows the differential and guides treatment/transport.
  • Differentiate shock states (cardiogenic, hypovolemic, obstructive, distributive) with a simple decision tree.
  • Know where POCUS changes diagnosis/management versus where outcome evidence is neutral.
  • Acquire core cardiac views and IVC/FAST/aorta/lung checks reliably in the field.
  • Document, hand over, and avoid common pitfalls (probes, windows, orientation, over-calling).

Why this matters (Evidence snapshot)

Evidence summaries guide practice; outcomes depend on definitive care.

Immediate goal-directed ultrasound improves early diagnostic accuracy.

An ED RCT showed correct “most-likely” diagnosis at 15 minutes increased substantially with immediate POCUS; differentials narrowed meaningfully.

POCUS often creates diagnostic shifts.

Protocols revealed time-critical conditions (e.g., PE, tamponade, AAA) that altered treatment and destination.

Strongest for obstructive shock.

Echocardiographic patterns (e.g., RV strain, effusion, absent lung sliding) help rule-in obstructive causes more reliably than mixed states.

No survival difference in a large trial.

A major ED RCT found no 30-day mortality improvement vs standard care—positioning POCUS as a diagnostic/triage enhancer.

Prehospital & HEMS feasible.

Field teams can complete a focused protocol and use results to adjust therapy and route to definitive care.

Protocol stepper: Focused Hypotension POCUS

Step 1: Cardiac (subcostal → parasternal long → apical 4-ch)

LV function, RV size, pericardial effusion/tamponade signs.

Cardiac views: Estimate global LV function (poor vs hyperdynamic), compare RV/LV size, look for pericardial effusion and tamponade physiology (RA/RV diastolic collapse).

Image/GIF placeholder (Cardiac views)

Step 2: IVC

Diameter & collapsibility (tiny/collapsible vs plethoric).

IVC: Small & collapsible suggests low volume; plethoric (>~2.5 cm) suggests high right-sided pressure/overload context. Interpret with respiratory mode in mind.

Image/GIF placeholder (IVC)

Step 3: FAST

RUQ, LUQ, suprapubic for free fluid.

FAST: Free fluid in RUQ/LUQ/pelvis supports hemorrhage; do not delay transport for exhaustive scans.

Image/GIF placeholder (FAST)

Step 4: Aorta

Proximal/mid/distal for AAA screen when indicated.

Aorta: Screen proximal/mid/distal for aneurysm/dilatation in undifferentiated hypotension at risk.

Image/GIF placeholder (Aorta)

Step 5: Lung (anterior)

Sliding vs absence; B-lines profile.

Lung: Presence/absence of sliding (consider pneumothorax). B-lines profile supports hydrostatic pulmonary edema vs obstructive/reactive disease patterns.

Decision tree (action cards)

Cardiogenic

Poor LV ± large IVC → pressor strategy, airway/ventilation, cath/ICU route.

  • Treat-now triggers: hypotension with low LV output
  • Consider pressors after preload optimization
  • Early cath/ICU destination

Hypovolemic

Hyperdynamic LV + tiny IVC → hemorrhage control, fluids/blood, trauma center.

  • Treat-now triggers: free fluid on FAST, tiny collapsible IVC
  • Hemorrhage control, TXA per local guidance
  • Fluids/blood and trauma-center routing

Obstructive (PE/Tamponade/PTX)

RV>LV, plethoric IVC, effusion, no sliding → treat-now actions & route to capable center.

  • PE pattern → thrombolysis/ECMO-capable consult
  • Tamponade → drain capability
  • Tension PTX → immediate decompression

Distributive

Normal heart, variable IVC, B-lines patterning → early antibiotics/source control/vasopressors.

  • Sepsis bundle (fluids, antibiotics, vasopressors)
  • Consider anaphylaxis pathway when indicated
  • Source control and destination planning

Checklist

Common pitfalls & fixes

  • “Can’t see the heart?” Try subcostal first, increase depth, adjust gain, patient positioning if safe.
  • “Weird orientation?” Confirm probe marker and screen indicator every view.
  • “Reading IVC in ventilated patient?” Integrate with cardiac/lung/clinical picture; avoid single-measure decisions.

Documentation & Handover

“Protocol completed,” “Key finding(s),” “Impact on destination/plan.”

“Focused hypotension POCUS completed: cardiac/IVC/FAST/aorta/lung. Findings: hyperdynamic LV, tiny collapsible IVC, no effusion, no free fluid, lung sliding present. Impression: hypovolemia likely. Action: hemorrhage control + blood en route. Destination: Major Trauma Center.”

POCUS Hypotension: Field Checklist

  • Cardiac → LV fxn / RV size / effusion
  • IVC → tiny & collapsible vs plethoric
  • FAST → RUQ/LUQ/pelvis fluid
  • Aorta → >3 cm? concerning
  • Lung → sliding? B-lines?
